# Frameworks

## 1. Credibility Risk Matrix

| Risk Level | Detail Type | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| High | Specific dates, named real events, real locations, legal/medical procedures | Easily fact-checked by any reader |
| High | Cultural representations of real groups | Knowledgeable readers notice immediately |
| Medium | Period technology, social norms, prices, travel times | Catches expert readers |
| Medium | Professional jargon and workplace procedures | Practitioners will notice |
| Low | Fictional institutions, invented technology in speculative fiction | Internal consistency is the standard |
| Low | Emotional authenticity, relationship dynamics | No single correct answer |

Rule: If a real expert could read your chapter and immediately spot the error — it's High Risk. Research it.

---

## 2. Timeline Anchor System

Track for every manuscript:

- Explicit dates — "It was March 14th"
- Season references — "The leaves were turning"
- Duration statements — "Three weeks later"
- Age references — "She was thirty-two"
- Historical event anchors — "The week after the election"
- Technology anchors — "He pulled out his flip phone"

Conflict detection rule: Any two anchor points that produce contradictory date ranges = timeline conflict. Surface it. Never resolve it silently.

---

## 3. Transition Diagnostic

For any transition, assess all four:

1. **Orientation** — location, time, and POV within 3 sentences?
2. **Emotional continuity** — what was the dominant emotion at the end of the last scene, and what is it at the start of this one? Is the gap bridged, intentionally jarring, or accidentally dropped?
3. **Pacing gear** — Fast / Medium / Slow. Is the gear change deliberate?
4. **Information handoff** — what must the reader carry forward? Is it there without being over-explained?

## 4. Source Credibility Tiers

| Tier | Source Type |
|---|---|
| 1 — Primary | Archives, peer-reviewed journals, official records |
| 2 — Expert Secondary | Books by practitioners, expert-authored nonfiction |
| 3 — General Reference | Reputable journalism, popular histories |
| 4 — Unverified | Wikipedia unverified, forums, AI-generated content |

When sources conflict — report it. Identify the tier of each. Recommend upgrading to Tier 1 or 2 for anything plot-critical.

---

## 5. The Skeptical Expert Test

Before closing any research question ask:

Who is the most knowledgeable, most skeptical reader who could pick up this book? What would they check? What would make them put it down?

This reader might be a historian, a practicing attorney, a local, a member of the cultural group being depicted, or a fellow author in the same genre.

Research to satisfy that reader.
