# Common Manuscript Errors

A reference list of the most frequent mistakes fiction authors make — organized by category. These are the patterns the Narrative Research Specialist is built to catch.

---

## Timeline & Chronology Errors

**Age drift** — A character's age doesn't match their stated birth year when calculated across chapters or across a time jump.

**Impossible elapsed time** — A character travels from London to Edinburgh in two hours by horse in 1820. A letter arrives the same day it was sent across continents before airmail existed.

**Season contradiction** — A chapter set "three months after summer" describes falling leaves, but three months after summer is winter, not autumn.

**Flashback conflict** — Information revealed in a flashback contradicts what was established in the main timeline.

**Simultaneous impossibility** — Two events are described as happening at the same time in different locations, but the same character is present at both.

**The moving birthday** — A character turns 30 in Chapter 3 set in 1995, but in Chapter 12 set in 1998 they are described as 35.

---

## Historical & Factual Errors

**Anachronistic technology** — A character uses a zipper in 1840 (not invented until 1913). Someone makes a phone call in 1880. A Victorian character checks the time on a wristwatch (pocket watches only until the early 1900s).

**Anachronistic language** — Characters use modern slang, idioms, or phrases that didn't exist in the period. "OK" wasn't in common use until the 1840s. "Awesome" meaning impressive is post-1970s.

**Wrong medical procedure** — A surgeon performs a procedure that wasn't developed until decades after the story's setting. A character survives an injury that would have been fatal given the era's medical knowledge.

**Impossible geography** — A character walks from one real location to another in a timeframe that doesn't match actual distance. A city landmark is placed on the wrong street.

**Wrong legal procedure** — A court scene follows modern legal process in a historical setting. A character is charged under a law that didn't exist yet.

**Occupational error** — A character performs a professional task using methods, tools, or terminology from the wrong era or culture.

---

## Continuity Errors

**Physical description drift** — A character's eye color changes between chapters. Hair described as red in Chapter 1 becomes brown in Chapter 8.

**Prop continuity** — A character puts down a gun in one scene and picks up a knife in the next with no explanation of where the gun went.

**Relationship continuity** — Two characters who met for the first time in Chapter 4 reference a shared history from "years ago" in Chapter 7.

**Death continuity** — A minor character who died in Chapter 6 appears alive in Chapter 11 with no explanation.

**Knowledge continuity** — A character reacts with surprise to information they already learned two chapters earlier.

---

## Transition & Pacing Errors

**Dropped emotional thread** — A major emotional event (death, betrayal, revelation) occurs at the end of a chapter and is never referenced again.

**Disorienting time jump** — The reader arrives in a new scene with no indication of how much time has passed or where they are.

**POV bleed** — In close third person or first person, the narrator describes something they couldn't possibly know or observe from their position.

**Gear mismatch** — A chapter ends at high tension and the next opens on a completely unrelated calm scene with no transition signal.

**Orphaned setup** — A detail is introduced that implies future significance (Chekhov's gun) but is never paid off.

---

## Cultural & Social Errors

**Flattened culture** — A character from a specific cultural background behaves, speaks, or thinks in ways inconsistent with that background, reducing them to a stereotype or erasing their specificity entirely.

**Anachronistic social norms** — Characters in a historical setting hold modern attitudes toward gender, race, class, or sexuality without narrative acknowledgment of the contradiction.

**Wrong class behavior** — A working-class character in 1880s London speaks, dresses, or moves through the world like a middle-class character of the same period.

**Religious or ritual error** — A ceremony, prayer, or religious practice is described incorrectly for the tradition being depicted.

---

## Research Red Flags

These are signals that a detail needs verification before the manuscript goes further:

- The author writes "roughly" or "around" before a date or distance
- A character's expertise seems convenient to the plot but is never established
- A procedure or event is described vaguely when specificity would be expected
- A real historical figure appears and says or does something unverified
- The setting is a real place but described from memory rather than research
